Seasonal Energy Management Strategies

Spring (March–May)
Spring is the lowest-cost season for energy in most climates. Mild temperatures reduce both heating and cooling loads. Use this period to: schedule HVAC maintenance before summer demand peaks drive up service costs; re-seal weatherstripping and caulk any air infiltration points; reprogram thermostat schedules from winter to shoulder-season settings (cooling setpoint can often be as high as 80°F/26.7°C when outdoor temperatures are below 70°F). Ceiling fans running counterclockwise at low speed improve comfort enough to raise the thermostat 3–4°F with no discomfort.

Summer (June–August)
Summer peak-demand periods drive the highest electricity rates. Key strategies:

Pre-cooling: In TOU-rate markets, drop thermostat to 74°F (23.3°C) before the 4 PM peak window, then let the home coast to 80°F (26.7°C) by 9 PM. This shifts 1–2 kWh of daily cooling load from peak ($0.28/kWh) to mid-peak or off-peak rates, saving $0.20–0.40 per cycle or roughly $15–30 per month.

Shade management: close blinds and curtains on south and west-facing windows from 11 AM onward. Internal window coverings reduce solar heat gain by 30–40%. Exterior shading (awnings, trees) is more effective, blocking heat before it reaches the glass.

Night-flush ventilation: in climates where overnight temperatures fall below 65°F (18.3°C), open windows after 10 PM and run whole-house fans to flush stored heat from the building mass. Close windows by 7 AM to seal in the cool. This technique can delay air conditioning onset by 2–3 hours.

Pool pump scheduling: shift pump runtime entirely to off-peak hours. An 8-hour filtration cycle run between 10 PM and 6 AM saves the full peak-rate differential. Running at night also reduces evaporative losses.

Autumn (September–November)
As temperatures moderate, reset thermostat schedules to avoid overcooling. Audit the year's peak charges on the electricity bill and identify the highest-consumption days to understand which loads should be deferred next summer. Service heat pumps before heating season begins.

Winter (December–February)
Heating loads dominate. Electric resistance heating (strip heaters, electric baseboards) is the most costly option — heat pumps are far more efficient down to roughly -5°F (-20°C) with cold-climate models. Useful strategies:

Setback scheduling: lower heating setpoint from 68°F (20°C) to 60°F (15.5°C) overnight or during away periods. For a well-insulated home, each 1°F reduction in setpoint saves approximately 1–3% on heating costs.

Water heater pre-heating: schedule the water heater to complete its heating cycle by 11 PM (within the off-peak window) so morning hot-water demand draws from stored hot water rather than triggering a morning-peak heating cycle.

Dryer usage: clothes dryers peak around 5 kW. Schedule drying cycles for off-peak evenings. Cleaning the lint filter before every load reduces drying time by 15–25%.

Solar generation is lower in winter due to lower sun angles and shorter days. Expect 40–60% of summer production in December–January for most U.S. locations. Configure battery storage (if present) to prioritize self-consumption from the previous day's solar rather than exporting.
